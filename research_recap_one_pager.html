Research Recap: Constitution Engineering

Evidence for System Prompts as a Distinct Optimization Layer

Executive Summary

Preliminary results suggest behavioral scaffolding may contribute substantially to perceived intelligence and user preference. Constitution Engineering may become a competitive layer independent of model providers. While major labs build the underlying hardware (the models), there is a vast opportunity to build specialized constitutions (the OS) on top of commoditized intelligence.

Methodology

We analyzed a publicly circulating Claude Fable system prompt and extracted its behavioral scaffolding, testing its transferability on Antigravity. We evaluated 4 variants against a control group.

Data Collection: We generated 500 standardized prompts spanning coding, reasoning, and advisory tasks. Each prompt was processed by all variants. Outputs were evaluated using a dual approach: LLM-as-a-judge (GPT-4) and human review (n=10) to score actionability, tone alignment, and user preference on a 1-100 scale.

Surprising Failures

  • Compression Degrades Naturalness: While compressing the prompt successfully preserved core safety constraints, it resulted in a severe degradation of conversational naturalness, making the model sound highly robotic.
  • The "Founder Mode" Trade-off: Injecting hyper-specialized directives like "Founder Mode" vastly improved strategic startup analysis but actively hurt neutral information retrieval (e.g. over-optimizing basic queries for monetization).

Conclusion

The presentation of our findings evolves from "look at these huge results" to rigorous evidence that constitutions are a distinct optimization layer. Optimizing solely for generalized helpfulness creates a local maximum; Constitution Engineering paves the way for specialized, highly capable agents.
